About Jow Ga
Kung Fu

Jow Ga — 周家拳, meaning Jow Family Boxing — is a rare and powerful form of Chinese martial art founded by Jow Lung, born in Guangdong province in 1891. It is known by the phrase Hung Tao Choy Mei — "Hung's Head and Choy's Tail" — reflecting its synthesis of two great systems.

Jow Lung's journey took him from his uncle's Hung Gar teachings, through the mines of Malaysia, to a mountain temple where the abbot Chian Yi taught him Northern Shaolin kung fu. Encouraged to merge everything he had mastered, Jow Lung spent three years in the monastery forging a completely new system.

In 1915, Jow Lung defeated all 100 competitors in an open tournament to become chief trainer to General Lee Fook Lam's army. With his brothers — known together as the Five Tigers of Jow Ga — they refined the system and built its legendary reputation.

Jow Ga is a complete martial art: powerful Hung Gar upper-body mechanics combined with Choy Gar's deceptive footwork and Northern Shaolin's long-range techniques. Students train hand forms, weapon forms, grappling, throwing, and the five animal techniques of tiger, leopard, dragon, crane, and snake.

"I want to teach good, honest people. There's no anger or arrogance. My class is a happy class." — Sifu Raymond Keung

Born in Hong Kong on 24 July 1959, Sifu Raymond began his Jow Ga journey in 1973 — encouraged by his father to join a kung fu school to build strength and overcome introversion. He found his calling.

Under Master Ho Lok, he built the essential foundation: correct body posture, punishing low stances, and the discipline that defines serious Jow Ga training. His dedication was exceptional. Seeking to deepen his knowledge, he sought out Master Yip Chi Wah — a direct descendant of Jow Biu's lineage — where he expanded his mastery of hand forms, weapon forms, and set fighting.

His remarkable memory led him to become known throughout the Jow Ga community as the "Dictionary of Jow Ga Forms" — a living repository of the art's complete technical heritage. This knowledge, spanning over 20 hand forms and extensive weapon sets, is what he now passes to his students in South Africa.

In 2003 Sifu Raymond immigrated to South Africa with his family, and soon established the Jow Ga Kung Fu Athletic Association of South Africa — a life member and representative of the International Jow Ga Kuen Federation. He interviews every prospective student personally. This is not a commercial operation. It is a living tradition.

Sifu Raymond also teaches Tai Chi, offering students the complementary art of controlled, meditative movement — the same attention to internal alignment and body mechanics that underpins his kung fu philosophy.

New Students

What to Expect

Do I need to be fit to start?

Not at all. Sifu Raymond welcomes students of all fitness levels. Regular training will develop your strength, flexibility, and stamina faster than you expect. Start where you are.

Do I need to call before my first class?

Yes — new students are asked to call ahead before attending. Sifu Raymond interviews prospective students personally. This is a deliberate part of the tradition, not a barrier.

What should I wear to my first class?

Comfortable, loose-fitting clothing that allows free movement. Barefoot or flat martial arts shoes. As you progress you may wish to acquire Fei Yue training shoes — we stock them.

How is Jow Ga different from other kung fu styles?

Jow Ga synthesises three complete systems — Hung Gar's powerful striking, Choy Gar's deceptive footwork, and Northern Shaolin's long-range techniques. It is a comprehensive, aggressive, and highly effective art, not a single-dimension style.

What does lineage mean, and why does it matter?

Lineage is the unbroken chain of knowledge passed from master to student across generations. Sifu Raymond is a 6th-generation direct descendant of Jow Lung's founding lineage through Jow Biu. What you learn here has been tested and refined over more than a century.

Can children and adults train together?

Jow Ga is suitable for all ages. Training is structured to challenge each student appropriately. Discipline, respect, and character development are central to the curriculum for younger students and adults alike.

Can I do Tai Chi without doing Kung Fu?

Absolutely. Tai Chi is taught as a complete practice in its own right. Many students come specifically for Tai Chi — whether for health, balance, stress relief, or internal development. All are welcome.

Is this affiliated with a wider organisation?

Yes. Sifu Raymond is a life member and South African representative of the International Jow Ga Kuen Federation, connecting our school to the global Jow Ga family and ensuring authentic lineage continuity.
